    Civic Transparency

    Verified Provenance & Behavior Transparency Standard

    • GitHub Source Repository
    • Hosted Documentation

    Welcome to the official documentation for the Civic Transparency project.

    Our Vision

    Build an open, nonpartisan, behavioral early-warning dashboard by:

    • Ingesting publicly available, ToS-compliant data from platforms with usable APIs.

    • Tracking bursts, synchrony, recycled media, sudden follower spikes without assessing message content.

    • Publishing aggregated event-level signals (e.g., High coordination spike on Platform X in Topic Y) rather than targeting individual accounts.

    • Providing toolkits for journalists, civic groups, and election officials to investigate further.

    Motivation

    Coordinated manipulation thrives when people can't see how a narrative is spreading or whether automation is involved.

    We do not judge truth and we do not police viewpoints. The system merely shows how content moves (provenance + behavior), so everyone can evaluate trends on equal footing.

    It's better for platform providers as it uses low-cost metadata and aggregated signals that reduce legal risk and operational burden.

    Proposal

    Platforms embed machine-readable provenance tags in every public post (account creation date bucket, automation flag, original vs reshared, and media provenance if available).

    Provide a public transparency endpoint (a low-cost API) where anyone can fetch aggregate counts of different behavior types for a given hashtag, topic, or trend.

    Personal accounts get privacy protections (e.g., creation date shown in broad ranges, not exact), but coordination patterns remain visible.

    Benefits for Platforms

    • Reduces liability: If election manipulation is later uncovered, they can show they provided full transparency data in compliance with law—shifting blame to the bad actors.

    • Improves trust & brand: Platforms can market themselves as platforms with clear transparency tools.

    • Fights fraud at lower cost: Third parties help detect bots, reducing the need for massive in-house moderation spend.

    • Cross-jurisdiction compliance: Works with EU DSA & other transparency laws already in force.

    Benefits for People

    • Easy public verification: Journalists, watchdogs, and civic-minded individuals can check how organic a trending topic is.

    • Lower disinformation amplification: Bad actors can't easily hide mass coordination without showing up in transparency data.

    • User choice: Individuals could filter their feeds based on automation levels or coordination scores.

    Core design

    Attach machine-readable provenance tags to every public post at creation.

    Host a public transparency endpoint that returns aggregated behavioral stats for a hashtag/topic/time window.

    Maintain privacy by design: fields are bucketed/ranged; no personal data; no DMs; no full content export.

    Key Aspects

    1. Journalists can spot abnormal account-age skew (e.g., a surge powered by week-old accounts).

    2. Watchdogs can flag synchrony spikes and recycled-content bursts without naming individuals.

    3. Users and researchers can compare automation rates across topics and dates.

    4. Privacy & neutrality safeguards - No personal information interchange. Buckets only; no exact join keys.

    5. Aggregate outputs only. Minimum group sizes (e.g., k-anonymity ≥ 100).

    6. Metrics are behavioral, not ideological. No viewpoint assessment.

    7. Appeals & audits. Platforms publish method notes and permit third-party audits of aggregation logic.

    8. Open methods. Public documentation of how burst/synchrony metrics are computed.

    Costs & Incentives

    • Tiny marginal compute: Tags are set once at post time; aggregation runs on compact counters/time series.

    • Caching + rate limits: Popular topic windows get cached; long tails are throttled.

    • Legal risk reduced: If manipulation later surfaces, platforms can show they provided standardized transparency, shifting liability to bad actors.

    • Trust dividend: Clear, neutral transparency—good for regulators, advertisers, and users.

    • Cross-jurisdiction fit: Cleanly aligns with transparency duties in laws like the EU's DSA without exporting content.

    Governance & standardization

    • Open spec steward: A neutral standards body (e.g., an IEEE/ISO working group or a new multi-stakeholder consortium) could manage the schema and metric definitions.

    • Versioned spec & model cards: Changes to buckets/metrics are versioned; platforms publish "transparency model cards" just like AI model cards.

    • Certification tier: Independent auditors could certify conformance (similar to SOC 2).

    What is Visible (and not Visible)

    Reveals: coordination patterns, automation mix, recycled media rates, account-age skews, provenance quality signals.

    Doesn't reveal: who said what, private messages, exact identities, sensitive client info, or "truth."

    Possible Failure Modes & Suggested Mitigations

    • Actors adapt: They can age accounts longer.

      • Mitigation: multiple signals (synchrony, duplication, client mix) make single-signal evasion costlier.

    • Topic gaming: Flood benign posts under a hashtag.

      • Mitigation: allow multi-query by URL/media hash and cross-topic anomaly comparisons.

    • Chilling concerns: Users fear being "scored."

      • Mitigation: only aggregates are public; per-post tags are for computation, not public shaming.

    • Platform under-implementation:

    • Mitigation: offer certification and provide public conformity reports.

    Success metrics

    • Short term: more newsrooms and watchdogs citing coordination metrics in coverage; earlier detection of inorganic surges.
    • Medium term: measured decline in high-risk patterns during elections (e.g., lower burst/synchrony without corresponding organic growth).
    • Long term: higher public trust measures tied to transparency availability; cross-platform adoption of the same spec.

    Implementation Plan

    1. Develop and test first on simulated system using GitHub Pages.
    2. Prototype on open platforms (e.g., BlueSky, Fediverse, Reddit) to validate schema, metrics, and caching costs.
    3. Publish the standard + open-source reference implementation (ingest > counters > API).
    4. Invite platform pilots with low-stakes topics (sports, entertainment) before elections/civic topics.
    5. Independent audit & red-team of the metrics for fairness and privacy leakage.
    6. Policy bridge: Present evidence to regulators showing low cost, high civic value > consider possible recommendations for large platforms.
